Summary
AI evaluations are fundamental to development and deployment decisions, yet they are surprisingly fragile. Small changes in phrasing, formatting, or incentives can drastically alter results, raising concerns about their reliability. This fragility undermines safety assessments, research reproducibility, and informed scaling decisions. To improve, we need clearer standardised procedures, greater transparency in reporting uncertainty, and a recognition that behavioural evals only provide a lower bound on model capabilities.

Evaluations underlie most parts of the AI development and deployment process. Given how fundamental they are, it is shocking just how fragile these evaluations are. In the following, I'll show how even very small changes to the evaluation setup lead to drastically different results, why this is a major problem, and how we might fix itat least partially.

What exactly are evaluations?

Generally speaking, evaluations (evals for short) is the process of assessing some AI system on some metric. This is purposely broad because you can evaluate a specific capability, like mathematical reasoning, or something entirely different like alignment to certain values. The goal is to learn about the capabilities, tendencies, and flaws of AI systems (Apollo Research, 2023).

Most evals make use of benchmarks to standardise this process. Benchmarking makes a statement about the likelihood of a model behaving in a specific way on a certain dataset. Some prominent examples include MMLU for testing multitask knowledge, TruthfulQA for assessing truthfulness, and BigBench for evaluating a broad range of capabilities. These benchmarks serve as standardised measuring sticks against which different models can be compared.

Why are evals so important?

AI model evaluations are essential for understanding and mitigating the risks associated with advanced AI systems. They inform safety-related decisions and underpin responsible scaling policies. For instance, evaluations help determine whether an AI system possesses potentially dangerous capabilities, guiding decisions on its deployment or restriction (Shevlane et al., 2023)

Evaluations serve multiple critical functions in AI development and deployment. At the most basic level, they provide safety assurance by helping assess potential risks before deployment. This includes examining a model's tendencies toward deception, its ability to generate harmful content, or its potential to assist in dangerous activities. Beyond safety, evaluations are crucial for understanding both intended and unintended capabilities that might emerge during training, which directly influences appropriate use cases and user expectations. During development, these assessments provide vital feedback that shapes training strategies and deployment readiness.

Helpful evals need to be robust

The importance of evaluation robustness becomes particularly acute when considering advanced AI systems that may approach or exceed human-level capabilities. When evaluations are fragile or inconsistent, they can lead to false confidence in model safety, missed safety concerns, and incorrect deployment decisions. This unreliability undermines our ability to make informed decisions about AI systems and their potential risks.

Furthermore, robust evaluations are fundamental to building a scientific understanding of AI systems. Without reliable evaluation methods, research findings become difficult to reproduce, progress becomes harder to measure, and meaningful comparisons between different models become impossible. This scientific uncertainty is particularly problematic for advanced AI systems, where the consequences of unreliable evaluations become increasingly severe.

For scaling decisions, evaluation results often inform choices about further model development. Unreliable evaluations could lead to premature scaling of potentially dangerous systems or missed opportunities to implement crucial safety measures. The stakes are especially high when dealing with models that might develop emergent capabilities or pose unprecedented risks.

This context makes the robustness problems in current evaluation approaches particularly concerning. When evaluations can be significantly altered by small changes in format or context, their utility for these critical functions becomes questionable. For instance, if a model's performance on safety-critical tasks can vary dramatically based on simple reformatting, how can we trust these evaluations to inform deployment decisions?

Whats wrong with evals?

Given this understanding of why robust evals are important, lets take a look at their current fragility in practice. Ill be focussing on evals high sensitivity to seemingly minor changes, and will make use of three case studies to illustrate this problem.

Case Study 1: Incentive Sensitivity

Recent work by Bsharat et al. (2023) demonstrated a striking phenomenon: offering hypothetical monetary incentives to language models significantly altered their behaviour and performance on standard benchmarks. In their experiments, simply adding text like "I'll give you $50 if you get this right" led to performance changes of up to 20 percentage points on standard reasoning tasks. This raises profound questions about how we should interpret model behaviour when it can be so dramatically influenced by hypothetical incentives that shouldn't affect a computational system.

One might argue that changing incentives represents a fundamental alteration of the task rather than a superficial change. However, this premise itself reveals a crucial problem: we lack a clear framework for determining what constitutes a meaningful change in evaluation context versus a superficial variation. Ideally, models should maintain consistent behaviour regardless of such hypothetical incentives, just as we would expect a calculator to produce the same results regardless of how much money we offer it.

Case Study 2: Synonymous Replacements

The problem becomes even more apparent when examining truly superficial changes. Mizrahi et al. (2023) conducted extensive experiments with synonymous replacements in evaluation prompts. They found that replacing phrases like "Select the correct answer" with semantically equivalent alternatives like "Choose the right response" led to statistically significant variations in model accuracy. Their work documented performance swings of nearly 100 percentage points across various benchmarks, despite maintaining identical task semantics.

Even more concerning was the inconsistency of these effects across different models and tasks. A synonymous replacement that improved performance on one benchmark might decrease it on another, making it impossible to establish "optimal" evaluation prompts that work consistently across different contexts.

To explore this effect, select different phrasings from the homophones task. Despite all instructions being semantically equivalent, both absolute performance and relative ranking vary widely. The same model (e.g. gpt-3.5-turbo) performs terribly on P3 but great on P4, despite minimal changes in phrasing.

Case Study 3: Formatting Variations

Perhaps the most troubling evidence comes from research on formatting sensitivity by Sclar et al. (2023). Their work systematically examined how changes in the presentation format of multiple-choice questions affected model performance. Despite preserving identical semantic content, these formatting changes led to significant performance variations on standard benchmarks. Sclar et al. documented these effects across multiple model architectures and evaluation datasets, suggesting this is a fundamental issue rather than a quirk of specific models or benchmarks.

The researchers also found that the impact of formatting changes wasn't consistent across different types of questions or models. This inconsistency makes it particularly challenging to establish standardized evaluation procedures, as optimal formatting choices might vary depending on the specific context.

These prompt-based issues, while concerning, represent just one dimension of evaluation fragility. The robustness problem extends far beyond prompt engineering to encompass the entire evaluation pipeline. Temperature settings and sampling parameters can dramatically affect results, while different input preprocessing methods and output parsing approaches introduce additional variability. Even environmental factors like context window management and batch processing can significantly impact evaluation outcomes. The cumulative effect is a complex web of potential variations that makes it challenging to draw reliable conclusions about model capabilities or safety properties.

Towards More Robust Evaluations

No single solution will eliminate all inconsistencies, but the following key improvements can make evaluations significantly more robust and reliable.

Standardised Evaluation Procedures

One of the most direct ways to improve evaluation reliability is through clearer and more standardised procedures. Recent work, such as the BetterBench paper, highlights the importance of rigorous, well-documented benchmarking methodologies (Reuel et al., 2024). Standardisation helps reduce variance caused by subtle implementation differences and ensures that results are more comparable across models, datasets, and research groups. This includes fixing formatting conventions, establishing predefined prompt variations, and enforcing consistency in experimental conditions.

Beyond standardisation, evaluations should also incorporate statistical best practices. Many current benchmarks report single-point accuracy without uncertainty estimates, making it difficult to assess result reliability. Reporting confidence intervals, statistical power analyses, and sensitivity studies would help surface the degree of variation in evaluation results, rather than obscuring it behind seemingly precise scores.

Recognising the Limits of Behavioural Evaluations

A critical step in improving evaluation practices is acknowledging their inherent limitations. Current behavioural evaluations only provide a lower bound on what a model is capable of achieving. As highlighted by Barnett and Thiergart (2024), failing an evaluation does not necessarily mean that a model is incapable of performing the taskit may simply indicate that the specific setup failed to elicit the behaviour. This is particularly relevant when assessing safety risks: just because a model does not exhibit a dangerous capability in an evaluation does not guarantee that it could not do so in different circumstances.

Recognising these limitations requires a shift in how evaluation results are interpreted. Rather than treating them as definitive measures of a models ability, they should be understood as probabilistic indicatorsuseful for assessing trends but insufficient as guarantees.

Transparent Reporting and Uncertainty Quantification

Transparency is essential for addressing evaluation fragility. This includes clearly documenting all evaluation choices, from prompt phrasing to data preprocessing steps, and making raw results available for replication. More importantly, evaluations should communicate uncertainty explicitly. If small changes in phrasing or formatting cause significant variations in performance, those variations should be reported rather than averaged away.

One approach is to move beyond single-metric reporting and present evaluation results as distributions, showcasing performance variability across different conditions. Additionally, when evaluations are used for decision-makingsuch as informing deployment policiesreports should explicitly state their limitations and the confidence level in the results.

While current evaluation practices have significant weaknesses, they are not beyond repair. By implementing clearer standardised procedures, acknowledging the limitations of behavioural assessments, and embracing transparency in reporting, we can build a more robust foundation for understanding AI capabilities and risks.
